\subsection{Clifford and T with Measurement}

Clifford + T computation are performed in the following three steps.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Prepare the states $\ket{0}^{\otimes n}$,
	\item Perform a sequence of Clifford + T gates,
	\item Measure in $Z$ basis.
\end{enumerate}

As shown in the the remark in section \ref{remark:alternating-pauli-clifford}, the gadget can be implemented with alternating Pauli measurements and Clifford gates on an extra ancilla qubit prepared in $\ket{T}$, where subsequent Pauli the Clifford gates only depend on the previous measurement outcomes.

Thus for a Clifford + T circuit, we can let the Clifford gates remain the same and replace each $T$ gate with the above Pauli measurement construction. 
As product of two clifford gate is still a clifford gate, the construction guarantees that there is a Pauli measurement between each clifford gate. 
We still need to make sure that no two Pauli measurements are next to each other. 
The only way this may take place is that there are two consective $T$ gates on the same qubit. 
However, two $T$ gates make an $S$ gate which is part of Clifford, so there is no such problem, and we conclude that alternating Pauli measurement with Clifford with ancilla $\ket{T}$ can simulate any Clifford + T circuit.

The number of $\ket{T}$ required is exactly equal to the number of $T$ gates in the original circuit.
In this way, we have simulated a Clifford + T circuit with alternating Pauli measurements and Clifford gates.

\subsection{Only Commuting Pauli}

We take the following lemma for granted
\begin{lemma}\label{lemma:clifford-non-commuting}
	For any non-commuting pauli $P$ and $Q$, there exists a Clifford $C$ such that $C P C^\dagger = X_1$ and $C Q C^\dagger = Z_1$. where $X_1$ and $Z_1$ are the Pauli $X$ and $Z$ acting on the first qubit.
\end{lemma}

Using this lemma, we can prove the following theorem

\begin{theorem}\label{theorem:non-commuting-pauli}
	Let $P, Q$ be two non-commuting Pauli. 
	Then $\Pi^j Q \Pi^i P \propto C_j \Pi^i P$ for some Clifford $C_j$ depending on the measurement outcome $j$ of $\Pi^j Q$.
	Moreover, $\Pi^j Q$ outcome following a $P$ measurement gives $\frac{1}{2}$ chance for each outcome $j$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
	Use lemma \ref{lemma:clifford-non-commuting}, we can find a Clifford $C$ such that $C P C^\dagger = X_1$ and $C Q C^\dagger = Z_1$. So we only need to verify the case when $P = X_1$ and $Q = Z_1$ with explicit calculation.
	In particular, we only need to verify the computation on the first qubit as the rest of the qubits are not affected by the measurement and Clifford gates.

	\begin{align*}
		\Pi^0 Z_1 \Pi^0 X_1 &= 
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} 
		\cdot 
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
		=
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}	
		\propto
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} 
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1	\end{pmatrix}
		= H \cdot \Pi^0 X_1
	\end{align*}
	\begin{align*}
		\Pi^1 Z_1 \Pi^0 X_1 &= 
		\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} 
		\cdot 
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
		=
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}	
		\propto
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} 
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1	\end{pmatrix}
		=  \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \Pi^0 X_1
	\end{align*}
	\begin{align*}
		\Pi^0 Z_1 \Pi^1 X_1 &= 
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} 
		\cdot 
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}
		=
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}	
		\propto
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} 
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -1	\end{pmatrix}
		= H \cdot \Pi^1 X_1
	\end{align*}
	\begin{align*}
		\Pi^1 Z_1 \Pi^1 X_1 &= 
		\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} 
		\cdot 
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}
		=
		\frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}	
		\propto
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} 
		\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1	\end{pmatrix}
		=  \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \Pi^1 X_1
	\end{align*}

	Notice that in all cases we can write $\Pi^i Z \Pi^j X = C_i \Pi^j X$ for some Clifford $C_i$ depending only on the measurement outcome $i$ of $\Pi^i Z$.

	We know that $\Pi^0 X \ket{\psi} = \ket{+} = \frac{\ket{0} + \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}$, by Born's rule the probability of outcome $j$ for $\Pi^j Z$ is $\bra{+} \Pi^j Z \ket{+} = \frac{1}{2}$ for both $j = 0, 1$. 
	(Note that we do not need to worry about $\Pi^0 X \ket{\psi} = 0$, as the probability of this measurement outcome is zero).
	For the exact same reasoning the probability of outcome $j$ for $\Pi^j Z$ is also $\frac{1}{2}$ when the input state is $\Pi^1 X \ket{\psi} \propto \ket{-} = \frac{\ket{0} - \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}$.
	This completes the proof.
\end{proof}

From the answer to the previous part we can simulate a Clifford + T circuit with only Pauli measurements with ancilla $\ket{T}$.
Assuming our sequence of measurements is $\Pi^{i_n}S_n, \cdots \Pi^{i_1}S_1$. (In this notation the first measurement is $\Pi^{i_1}S_1$ and the last measurement is $\Pi^{i_n}S_n$.)
We can start with $k = 2$ and check if $\Pi^{i_k} S_i$ commutes with all of the previous measurements $\Pi^{i_1}S_1, \cdots, \Pi^{i_{k-1}}S_{k-1}$.
If it does we preceed to the next $k$. 
If it does not, $S_k$ must anticommute with at least one of the previous measurement, which we label as $P$.
Since all of the previous measurements commute with each other, we and move the measurement $P$ to right before $\Pi^{i_k} S_k$ without affecting anything.
Now use theorem \ref{theorem:non-commuting-pauli} to replace $\Pi^{i_k} S_k \Pi^j P$ with $C_j \Pi^j P$ for some Clifford $C_j$ depending on the measurement outcome $j$ of $\Pi^j P$. We know the measurement outcome $j$ is $\frac{1}{2}$ for each $j$ by theorem regardless of the input state, so we may just pick randomly $C_0$ and $C_1$ with probability $\frac{1}{2}$ each.
This extra Clifford gate can be moved to the end of the circuit and be absorbed into the final measurement as we have done in the previous section.

The algorithm is shown in detail in \ref{alg:commuting-pauli}

\begin{algorithm}\label{alg:commuting-pauli}
	\caption{Cliffort + T with commuting Pauli measurements}
	\hspace*{\algorithmicindent} \textbf{Input:} A Clifford + T circuit 

	\hspace*{\algorithmicindent} \textbf{Output:} A series of commuting Pauli measurement on $\ket{0} \otimes \ket{T}$ that simulates the given Clifford + T circuit.
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
	\State Use algorithm from previous section to find a series of Pauli measurements $\Pi^{i_n}S_n, \cdots \Pi^{i_1}S_1$ with ancilla $\ket{T}$ that simulates the given Clifford + T circuit.
	\State $R \gets \Pi^{i_1} S_1$ \Comment{$R$ is our final output of commuting Paulis}
	\State $k \gets 2$
	\While{$k \leq n$}
	\If{$\Pi^{i_k} S_k$ commutes with all the measurements in $R$} 
		\State $R \gets \Pi^{i_k} S_k R$
	\Else
	\State Let $P$ be the measurement in $R$ that does not commute with $\Pi^{i_k} S_k$.
	\State Use theorem \ref{theorem:non-commuting-pauli} to find Clifford $C_j$ such that $\Pi^{i_k} S_k \Pi^j P \propto C_j \Pi^j P$ for $j = 0, 1$.
	\State Pick randomly $C_j$ with probability $\frac{1}{2}$ for each $j$.
	\State Move $C_j$ to the end of the circuit and absorb it into the final measurement. (In this process all the pauli measurements $\Pi^{i_{m}} S_m$ for $m > k$ are changed accordingly to $C_j$.
	\EndIf
	\State $k \gets k + 1$
	\EndWhile 
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Only \texorpdfstring{$\ket{T}$}{}}

It is possible to simulate Clifford + T circuit with an equivalent circuit of commuting Pauli measurements with initial states in $\ket{T}^{k}$ by the following way.



Given a given Clifford + T circuit on $n$ qubits, we can use results from the previous section to simulate it with a series of (not necessarily commuting) Pauli measurements with $k$ ancilla $\ket{T}$.

First we shall note the, adding a series of $Z$ measurement at the beginning of of the circuit on the qubits of initial state $\ket{0}$ does not change the final measurement outcome, as the input state $\ket{0}$ is the eigenvector of $Z$ with eigenvalue $1$.
