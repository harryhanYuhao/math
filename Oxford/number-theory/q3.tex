\section{Q3}

In this section, we investigate what happens if $p | h(\Qd)$.

\subsection{Coprime Solutions}

Assuming $x, y$ are coprime solutions for the equation 
$$
	y^p = x^2 + r^2 d
$$
but this time $p | h(\Qd)$.
Assuming $C_1, C_2, \cdots, C_m$ are the ideal classes of $\Qd$ whose $p$-th power is the principal class.
For each ideal class $C_i$, we can find an ideal $\b_i$ which is the inverse of $C_i$.
Without loss of any generosity, let $\a \in C_i$. 
We have $1 = ([\b_i \a])^p = [\b_i]^p[\a]^p = [\b_i]^{p}$.
This means $\b_i^p$ is a principal ideal.

Let us denote this set as $\mathfrak{B}:= \{\b_1, \cdots, \b_m\}$ and define 
$\mathcal{G} = \{\gamma_1, \cdots, \gamma_m\}$ where $\langle \gamma_i\rangle = \b_i^p$.

By the exact same reasoning as in the proof of question 1, we write
$$
	y^p = (x + r\sqrt{-d})(x - r\sqrt{-d})
$$
where $\langle x+ r\smd \rangle$ and $\langle x - r\smd \rangle$ are coprime ideals, and 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:q3-factor}
	\langle x + r\smd \rangle = \a^p
\end{equation}
for some ideal $\a$.
Since $p | h(\Qd)$, we can not conclude directly that $\a$ is principal.
However, we know $\a^p$ is a principal ideal, so $\a \in C_i$  for some $i$.

Let $\b_i \in \mathfrak{B}$ be the inverse of $\a$.
This means $\c\a = \langle \delta \rangle$ for some $\delta \in \O$.
We have also shown that $\b_i^p = \langle \gamma_i \rangle$.
Therefore, multiplying both sides of \eqref{eq:q3-factor} by $\b_i^p$ gives 
$$
	\langle \gamma_i \rangle \langle x + r\smd \rangle = (\b_i\a)^p = \langle \delta \rangle^p
$$
which means 
$$
	\gamma_i (x + r\smd) = \delta^p
$$
This is our desired result.

\subsection{Non-coprime Solutions}

We can adopt similar measures as in Q2 to tackle the non-coprime solutions case. 

As in Q2, if $x, y$ are non-coprime solutions with $gcd(x, y) = c$, we can write 
$$
	\alpha_i \langle x + r \smd \rangle = \h ^p
$$
for some ideal $\h$ and some constant $\alpha$ that depends only on the common factor $c$ (thus depend only on the factors of $r$).
Now, $\h^p$ is a principal ideal, so $\h$ is in some ideal class $C_j$.
Let $\b_i$ be the inverse of $C_j$ and $\gamma_j$ be the element such that $\langle \gamma_j \rangle = \b_i^p$
Then we have
$$
\alpha_i \gamma_j \langle x + r\smd \rangle= (\b_i \h)^p = \langle \delta \rangle^p
$$
or some $\delta \in \O$.
This means 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:q3-non-coprime}
	\alpha_i \gamma_j (x + r\smd) = \delta^p
\end{equation}
which is our desired result.

Using exactly the same argument as in \autoref{sec:homogeneous},
\autoref{eq:q3-non-coprime} can be transformed into an equivalent homogeneous equation on two variables called the Thue equation, which can be efficiently solved. 

Since there are only finitely many choices for $\alpha_i$ and $\gamma_j$ (in our deduction, $\alpha_i$ and $\gamma_j$ only depends on $r$ and the structure of $\Qd$),
we can solve all the corresponding Thue equations corresponding to \autoref{eq:q3-non-coprime} and check if its solution is indeed our desired solution for \autoref{eq:main}.

\subsection{Solution for \texorpdfstring{$y^3 = x^2 + 26$}{}}

Let us find out the structure of the class group of $\Q(\sqrt{-26})$.
The discriminant of $\Q(\sqrt{-26})$ is $4 \cdot -26 = 104$.
The Minkowski bound is $M_{st} \sqrt{104} \approx 0.63663 \sqrt{104} \approx 6.49$. 
So we only need to check the prime ideals that divide $2, 3,$ and $5$.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Prime $2$ is ramified, and $\langle 2 \rangle = \langle 2, \sqrt{-26}\rangle^2$
	\item In the field $\Z / 3\Z$, $x^2 + 26 \equiv x^2 +2 \equiv (x+1)(x+2)$. Applying Dedekind's criterion, $\langle 3 \rangle = \langle 3, \sqrt{-26} + 1\rangle \langle 3, \sqrt{-26} + 2\rangle$. 
		Set $\a = \langle 3, \sqrt{-26} + 1\rangle$ $\b = \langle 3, \sqrt{-26} + 2\rangle$.
		We have $\a^2 = \langle 9, \sqrt{-26} + 1\rangle$ and $\a^3 = \langle \sqrt{-26} + 1\rangle$ which is principal.
		This also means $\a^2 \sim \b$.
	\item In the field $\Z / 5\Z$, $x^2 + 26 \equiv x^2 + 1 = (x+3)(x+2)$, so $\langle 5\rangle = \langle 5, 3 + \sqrt{-26}\rangle \langle 5, 2 + \sqrt{ 26}\rangle$.
		Write $ \c = \langle 5, 2 + \sqrt{-26}\rangle $. Note that $\c^2= \langle 25, 7 + \sqrt{-26} \rangle$ 
		and 
		$$
		\langle 25, \sqrt{-26} + 7\rangle \langle 7 - \sqrt{-26}\rangle = 25 \langle 3, \sqrt{-26} + 2\rangle 
		$$
		So $\c^2 \sim \b$.
		
		Also note that $\c^3 = \langle 125, \sqrt{-26} + 82\rangle$, and 
		$$
		2 \langle 125, \sqrt{-26} + 82\rangle = \langle 4 - 3 \sqrt{-26 }\rangle \langle 2, \sqrt{-26}\rangle
		$$
		so $\c^3 \sim \langle 2, \sqrt{26}\rangle$.
\end{enumerate}
In summary we see that class group of $\Q(\sqrt{-26})$ is generated by $\c = \langle 5, 2 + \sqrt{-26}\rangle $ and $h(\Q(\sqrt{-26})) = 6$.

There are three elements of the class group whose cube is the principal class, which are $\langle 1 \rangle$, $\c^2$, and $\c^4$.
We consider these cases separately.

\textbf{Case 1: $\a \sim \langle 1 \rangle$}

In this case, we have 
$$
x + \sqrt{-26} = \alpha^3 \quad \text{for some } \alpha \in \O_{\Q(\sqrt{-26})}
$$
Write $\alpha = u + v \sqrt{-26} $ for $u, v \in \Z$ gives
$$
x + \sqrt{-26} = u^3 + 3 u^2 v \sqrt{-26} - 78 uv^2 - 26 v^3 \sqrt{-26}
$$
so 
$$
v(3 u^2 - 26 v^2) = 1
$$
and $v =  1$ and $u = \pm 3$, and $y = (3 + \sqrt{-26}) (3- \sqrt{-26}) = 35$ and $x = \pm (u^3 - 78 uv^2) = \pm 207$.

\textbf{Case 2: $\a \sim \c^2$}

The inverse of $\c^2$ is $\c^4$, and $(\c^4)^3 = \c^{12} = \langle 8137 + 2616 \sqrt{-26} \rangle$, and $\gamma = 8137 + 2616 \sqrt{-26}$.
Our equation becomes 
\begin{align*}
	(8137 + 2616 \sqrt{-26})(x + \sqrt{-26})  &= \sqrt{-26}(2616x + 8137)+ 8137 x - 68016\\
											  &= (u + v \sqrt{-2 6})^3\\
	&=  (-26v^3 + 3 u^2 v) \sqrt{-26} + u^3 - 78 uv^2 
\end{align*}
This gives 
$G(u, v) = -26v^3 + 3 u^2 v = 2616x +8137$ and $F(u, v) = u^3 - 78uv^2 = 8137x - 68016$.
While we have Thue's Equation 
$$
	G(u, v) 8137 - F(u, v) 2616 = 8137^2 + 2616 ^2 \cdot 26
$$

SageMath's Thue equation solver states that the only solution is
$u = -43, v  = 1$, which gives $x = -1$ and $y = 27$.

\textbf{Case 3: $\a \sim \c^4$}

The inverse of $\c^4$ is $\c^2$, and $(\c^2)^3 = \c^{6} = \langle 109 + 12 \sqrt{-26} \rangle$, and $\gamma = 109 + 12 \sqrt{-26}$.
Our equation becomes 
\begin{align*}
	(109 + 12 \sqrt{-26})(x + \sqrt{-26})  &= \sqrt{-26}(12 x + 109)+ 109 x - 12 \cdot 26\\
											  &= (u + v \sqrt{-2 6})^3\\
	&=  (-26v^3 + 3 u^2 v) \sqrt{-26} + u^3 - 78 uv^2 
\end{align*}
This gives 
$G(u, v) = -26v^3 + 3 u^2 v = 12 x  +109$ and $F(u, v) = u^3 - 78uv^2 = 109 x - 12 \cdot 26$.
This gives Thue's Equation 
$$
	G(u, v) 109 - F(u, v) 12 = 109^2 + 12 ^2 \cdot 26
$$

SageMath's Thue equation solver states that the only solution is 
$u = 7, v =1$. 
Substitute back to $g$ gives $g(u, v)=121 \implies x = 1$ and $y = 27$.


In conclusion, the only solutions are $(\pm 1, 3)$ and $(\pm 207, 35)$.
