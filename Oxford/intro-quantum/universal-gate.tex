\section{Brief Introduction to Quantum Computation}

Computations, regardless of how they are performed, are, at their most fundamental level, physical processes. 
Humans perform computations in our mind by the biochemical process that takes place in the brain, classical computers by transmitting electronic signals in the circuit boards, and \textit{quantum computers} by manipulating and measuring the states of quantum mechanical objects \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.

The following postulates \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010} of quantum mechanics are key to our discussions.
\begin{enumerate}[label={\textbf{Postulate} \arabic*}]
	\item The simplest quantum state is \textit{qubit}, which is described by unit vector in $\mathbb{C}^2$;
	\item composition of an $n$-qubit state is described by unit vector in $(\mathbb{C}^2)^{\otimes n} \cong \mathbb{C}^{2^n}$;
	\item states evolved by unitary\footnote{$U$ is unitary if $U^\dagger U = I$} operations.
	\item quantum measurement is governed by the Born rule.
\end{enumerate}

We will discuss the first three postulates here, while leaving the discussion of the Born rule to section \ref{sec:measurement}.

Let $\ket{1} = \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{pmatrix}$ and $\ket{0} = \begin{pmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{pmatrix}$ be the standard orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{C}^2$.
Any one qubit state $\ket{\psi}$, therefore, can be described as 
$$
\ket{\psi} = a\ket{0} + b\ket{1}, \quad a, b \in \mathbb{C}, |a|^2 + |b|^2 = 1
$$

The following unitary operators are called Pauli operators, which will be important for later discussions and are presented here as examples of unitary operations.
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pauli}
	X = \begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1\\
1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -i\\
i & 0
\end{pmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\
0 & -1
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

It is worth discussing why states evolve according to unitary matrices.
The time evolution of a closed quantum state $\ket{\psi}$ is described by the \textit{Schrödinger equation}

\begin{equation}\label{schrodinger}
	i \hslash \frac{d \ket{\psi}}{d t} = H \ket{\psi}
\end{equation}
If $H$ is a time-independent Hamiltonian, the solution to \autoref{schrodinger} is 
$$
\ket{\psi(t)} = e^{\frac{-iH t}{\hslash}} \ket{\psi(0)}
$$
where $\ket{\psi(0)}$ is the initial state of the system.
Standard results from linear algebra show that for Hermitian $H$, $e^{\frac{-i H t}{\hslash}}$ is indeed unitary for any real $t$ \footnote{See section 2.2.2 of \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.}.

The existence of qubit systems in nature has been demonstrated by the famous Stern-Gerlach experiment \cite{Sakurai_Napolitano_2020}. 
Various physical realisations of quantum computers have been proposed, such as those based on ion traps \cite{steane1997ion}, optical photons \cite{gardiner2004quantum}, and nuclear magnetic resonance \cite{nmr}, among others.

The studies of the theory of quantum computation have shown very promising results. 
For example, many quantum algorithms, such as Shor's algorithm for factoring \cite{Shor_1997}, have been shown to be exponentially faster than the best known classical algorithms for the same problems.

In this article, we will regard the qubit and its unitary evolution as our fundamental quantum mechanical system.
We will assume that an ideal quantum computer exists and only discuss the theories of unitary operations.
The remaining paragraphs of this section are a brief introduction to quantum gates.
The rest of the article will focus on a particular set of quantum gates, namely the Clifford and T gates, and their ability to approximate any single-qubit unitary operation.

\subsection{Single Qubit Operation} \label{sec:bloch-sphere}

The Bloch sphere representation of a qubit is very helpful for our discussion.
Any qubit $\ket{\psi}$ can be written as $\ket{\psi} = a \ket{0} + b \ket{1}$ for some $a , b \in \mathbb{C}$ such that $|a|^2 + |b|^2 = 1$.
If we write $|a| = \cos \frac{\theta}{2}$ and $|b| = \sin \frac{\theta}{2}$, then for some phase factors $e^{i \alpha}$ and $e^{i \beta}$, we have
$$
\ket{\psi} = e^{i \beta} \left(\cos \frac{\theta}{2} \ket{0} + e^{i \alpha} \sin \frac{\theta}{2} \ket{1}\right),
$$

We can represent $\ket{\psi}$ as a point on the unit sphere, where $\theta$ and $\alpha$ denote the polar and azimuthal angles. 
This is called a Bloch sphere.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{./figures/bloch.png}
	\caption{Bloch Sphere}
	\label{fig:Bloch Sphere}
\end{figure}

The action of Pauli $Z$ from \eqref{eq:pauli} on the block sphere is.
\begin{dmath*}
	Z \ket{\psi} = Z \ket{\psi} 
	= e^{i \beta} \left(\cos \frac{\theta}{2} \ket{0} - e^{i \alpha} \sin \frac{\theta}{2} \ket{1}\right)
	= e^{i \beta} \left(\cos \frac{\theta}{2} \ket{0} + e^{i \alpha + \pi} \sin \frac{\theta}{2} \ket{1}\right)
\end{dmath*}
It transforms a point on the Bloch sphere from $(\theta, \alpha)$ to $(\theta, \alpha + \pi)$, which is the rotation by $\pi$ on the $z$ axis.
By similar arguments, we can show Pauli $X, Y$ in \eqref{eq:pauli} correspond to the rotations of $\pi$ along the $x, y$ axis \footnote{See section 4.2 of \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.}.
It turns out that unitary operations on a single qubit are precisely the rotations on the Bloch sphere.

\begin{theorem}[Unitaries on Bloch Sphere]\label{th:unitary-bloch}
	The actions of single-qubit unitary matrices are equivalent to rotation (represented by $SO(3)$) in the Bloch sphere.
\end{theorem}
This theorem is Exercise 4.6 from \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.


\subsection{Measurement}\label{sec:measurement}

Our discussion of measurement is brief and only for the sake of completeness. 
For more details, see chapters 2.2 and 4.4 of \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.

Recall that a positive semidefinite projector is a Hermitian matrix $M$ such that $M^2 = M$ and $\bra{\psi} M \ket{\psi} \geq 0$ for any $\ket{\psi}$. 
Using the spectral theorem, we can show that each positive semidefinite projector $M$ can be written as
$$M = \sum_{i} c_i\ket{v_{i}} \bra{v_{i}}, \quad c_i \in \{0, 1\}$$
where $\ket{v_{i}}$ is a set of orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{C}^2$.
That is, the eigenvalues of a positive semidefinite projector are either 0 or 1. 

Let $\mathcal{M} = \{M_1, M_2, \cdots, M_n\}$ be a set of positive semidefinite projectors $V \rightarrow V$ such that $\sum_{i=1}^n M_i = I$. 
Each of $M_i$ is called an observable of the measurement $\mathcal{M}$.
Given a state $\kp$, the probability of obtaining the outcome $i$ is $\bra{\psi} M_i \ket{\psi}$, and the post-measurement state is $\frac{M_i \ket{\psi}}{\sqrt{\bra{\psi} M_i \ket{\psi}}}$.

This principle of obtaining probabilistic outcomes from measurement is called the Born rule.

From Born rule, we see $\kp$ and $e^{i\pi \theta} \kp$ are indistinguishable for any $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, since 
$$
\bra{\psi} M_i \ket{\psi} = \bra{\psi} e^{-i\pi \theta} M_i e^{i\pi \theta} \ket{\psi}
$$


\subsection{Univeral Gates}

In the effort to build quantum computers, one task is especially important: to find a small set of unitary matrices that can effectively simulate any unitary operation.
These unitary matrices are called universal gates.

Let us first concentrate on the single-qubit unitary operations.
There are infinitely many single-qubit unitary operations, making it impossible to exactly synthesize any arbitrary operation with a finite set of gates. 
\footnote{On classical computers, it is well known that the NAND gate alone is sufficient to implement any Boolean function \cite{sheffer1913set}.}
Therefore, we relax our goal to find a finite set of gates that can approximate any single qubit unitary operation with an arbitrarily small error in the sense defined below. 

\begin{definition}[Error of approximation]
	If $U$ is the unitary operation we wish to implement, while $V$ is the actual implementation in practice. 
	The \emph{error} of approximation is defined as  
	\begin{equation}
		E(U, V) = \max_{\ket{\psi}} \frac{\| (U - V) \ket{\psi} \|}{\| \ket{\psi} \|}.
	\end{equation}
\end{definition}

This definition of error has two important properties that fit our intuitions for errors. 
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Let $\ket{\psi}$ be our initial state. If $E(U, V)$ is small, the probability of obtaining different measurement outcomes from $U \kp$ and $V \kp$ is small;
	\item accumulated error satisfies the triangular inequality, that is 
		$$
			E(U_1 U_2, V_1, V_2) \leq E(U_1, V_1) + E(U_2, V_2)
		$$
\end{enumerate}

Their proofs are presented in the appendix \ref{sec:error}.

With the proper definition for error, we can settle the definition of a universal gate.
\begin{definition}[Universal Gate]
A set of quantum gates is called \emph{universal} if any unitary operation can be approximated with arbitrarily small error by a finite sequence of gates from the set.
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Universality of Clifford + T}

An important set of universal gates that we discuss in detail here is the Clifford + T gate. 
In the single qubit case, the Clifford + T gate set is generated by the Hadamard gate (denoted as $H$) and the T gate defined as
\begin{equation*}
		H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix}
		1 & 1\\
		1 & -1
		\end{pmatrix}, \quad T = \begin{pmatrix}
		1 & 0\\
		0 & e^{i\pi/4}
		\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation*}

\begin{remark}
	Note that both of $T$ and $H$ are unitary, and $T^4 = Z$ and $HZH = X$.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}
	The set $\{H, T\}$ is a universal gate set for single-qubit unitary operations.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
	This proof is based on section 4.5.3 of \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.

	We can write
	$$
	T = \cos \frac{\pi}{8} I - i \sin \frac{\pi}{8} Z, \quad H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (X + Z).
	$$
	where $X, Z$ are the Pauli X and Z operators, respectively. 
	Calculation shows that 
	$$
	THTH = \cos^2\frac{\pi}{8} I - i \sin \frac{\pi}{8} \left( \cos \frac{\pi}{8} (X + Z) + \sin \frac{\pi}{8} Y \right),
	$$
	This is rotation along the axis $(\cos\frac{\pi}{8}, \sin\frac{\pi}{8}, \cos\frac{\pi}{8})$ through an angle $\theta$ defined by $\cos \frac{\theta}{2} = \cos^2 \frac{\pi}{8}$.
	We can show that $\theta$ is a irrational multiple of $\pi$\footnote{See \cite{boykin1999} and section 4.5.3 of \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}. }.
	Therefore repeated applications of $THTH$ can approximate any rotation along the axis $(\cos\frac{\pi}{8}, \sin\frac{\pi}{8}, \cos\frac{\pi}{8})$ to arbitrary precision.

	By the same calculation we can show that $HTHT$ is a rotation along the axis $(\cos\frac{\pi}{8}, -\sin\frac{\pi}{8}, \cos\frac{\pi}{8})$ through the same angle $\theta$. 
	Repeated applications of $HTHT$ can approximate any rotation along the axis $(\cos\frac{\pi}{8}, -\sin\frac{\pi}{8}, \cos\frac{\pi}{8})$ to arbitrary precision.

	By a standard result in linear algebra, any single qubit unitary $U$ can be represented as
	$$
	U = R_{\hat{n}}(\alpha) R_{\hat{m}}(\beta) R_{\hat{n}}(\gamma),
	$$
	where $\hat{n}$ and $\hat{m}$ are two non-parallel axes, and $R_{\hat{n}}(\alpha)$ denotes the rotation along axis $\hat{n}$ through angle $\alpha$. \footnote{
		To quickly convince yourself of this fact, note that $R_{\hat{n}}(\alpha) R_{\hat{m}}(\beta) R_{\hat{n}}(-\alpha)$ of rotation along $\hat{k}$ through angle $\beta$, where the axis $\hat{k}$ is obtained rotating $\hat{m}$ along $\hat{n}$ through angle $-\alpha$.
	}

	Previous paragraphs shows that for any $\epsilon > 0$, $H$ and $T$ can be used to approximate generate $V_1, V_2, V_3$ such that $E(R_{\hat{n}}(\alpha), V_1) < \epsilon$, $E(R_{\hat{m}}(\beta), V_2) < \epsilon$ and $E(R_{\hat{n}}(\gamma), V_3) < \epsilon$.
	Applying proposition \ref{prop:cumulation-of-error} we see $E(U, V_1 V_2 V_3) < 3 \epsilon$, proving the universality of $H$ and $T$.
\end{proof}

So $H$ and $T$ gates can approximate any single-qubit unitary operation.
The surprising fact is that by adding one more gate, CNOT, they can be used to approximate any unitary operation on any number of qubits. 

\begin{theorem}
	Single-qubit gates together with the CNOT gate are a universal gate set for quantum computation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
	The detailed proof can be found here \cite{barenco1995elementary}.
	The gist of the proof is two parts 
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item any multi-qubit unitary operations can be decomposed into two-body unitary operations via Gaussian elimination. \footnote{By two-body unitary operation, we mean a unitary operation that acts non-trivially on only a 2-dimensional subspace.} 
		\item any two-body unitary operation can be implemented by CNOT and single-qubit gates.
	\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

The circuits generated by $H, S:=T^2$ and CNOT are called \emph{Clifford circuits}.
Notice we have the following algebraic relations 
\begin{equation*}
	\begin{aligned}
		H^2 = I, \quad S^4 = I, \quad (HS)^3 = e^{i\pi/4} I, \\
		H X H = Z, \quad H Z H = X, \quad S X S^{-1} = Y, \quad S Z S^{-1} = Z\\ 
		 XY = i Z, \quad YZ = i X, \quad ZX = i Y
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}

Because of these algebraic structures, Clifford circuits can be efficiently simulated on classical computers \cite{nest2009classicalsimulationquantumcomputation}.
This is the famous Gottesman-Knill theorem \cite{gottesman1998heisenbergrepresentationquantumcomputers}.

Combining the above discussion, we arrive at the following theorem. 

\begin{theorem}
	Clifford + T circuits are universal. 
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Solovay-Kitaev Theorem}

The natural question now is to ask, given an error bound $\epsilon$, how many gates do we need to approximate a single qubit unitary operation with Clifford and T gates?
Solovay-Kitaev algorithm \cite{dawson2005solovaykitaevalgorithm} gives an upper bound for the number of gates needed as $O(\log^c(1/\epsilon))$, where $c \approx 3.97$.

The algorithm improves approximations iteratively. 
Here is a brief summary of the algorithm.

Let $U_n$ be the $n$-th order approximation.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Base Case ($n=0$):} Find $U_0$ from a pre-computed $\epsilon_0$-net such that $\|U - U_0\| < \epsilon_0$.
    \item \textbf{Decomposition:} Define the error $\Delta = U U_n^\dagger$. Decompose $\Delta$ as a group commutator:
    \[ \Delta = V W V^\dagger W^\dagger \]
    where $V, W$ are unitaries such that $\|V-I\|, \|W-I\| \approx \sqrt{\| \Delta - I \|}$.
    \item \textbf{Recursion:} Compute $V_{n-1}$ and $W_{n-1}$ by applying the algorithm to $V$ and $W$ at the previous level.
    \item \textbf{Update:} Set $U_{n+1} = V_{n-1} W_{n-1} V_{n-1}^\dagger W_{n-1}^\dagger U_n$.
\end{enumerate}

In fact, the Solovay-Kitaev algorithm only requires the given set of gates to generate a dense subset of unitary matrices, so it can be applied to any universal gate set.

In the later section, we will demonstrate a more effective algorithm for approximating single-qubit unitaries with Clifford and T gates.

\subsection{Fault Tolerant Construction}

In practice, no quantum circuit can be implemented without errors.
A common approach to mitigate errors is through the use of quantum error correcting codes.
The theory of quantum error correcting codes is vast and advanced, but the gist of it is to encode a single logical qubit into a large number of physical qubits. 
When the error rate is below a certain threshold, the error can be detected and corrected \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}.  
This construction is called \textit{fault-tolerant construction}.

Clifford circuits, which are generated by the gates $H$, $S$, and CNOT, can be implemented fault-tolerantly \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}. 
Moreover, as they can be efficiently simulated on classical computers, various tricks can be used to make their physical implementation very cheap and efficient. 
On the other hand, fault-tolerant implementation of the T gate is much more expensive, and it is the main bottleneck for the construction of quantum computers \cite{Nielsen_Chuang_2010}. 

In the rest of this article, we will discuss practical algorithms for approximating single-qubit unitary operations with Clifford and T gates. 
We also touch on Matsumoto-Amano normal form, which can produce the minimal T-gate counts for any given Clifford + T circuit.
